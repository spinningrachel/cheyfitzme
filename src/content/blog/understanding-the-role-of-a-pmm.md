Nobody quite agrees on what a PMM does. That's the job's defining feature and its biggest liability.

Ask a product manager: "They run launches." Ask a demand gen marketer: "They write the messaging doc." Ask a founder: "They do... strategy? Sort of?" Ask a PMM: "Everything nobody else wants to own."

There's truth in all of those answers. There's also a lot of confusion that costs companies real money.

## What the role actually covers

A Product Marketing Manager lives at the intersection of product, marketing, and customer. They don't own any one of those domains fully — they translate between them.

The core responsibilities look like this:

**Positioning and messaging.** The PMM defines how the product gets described to the world. Not the tagline (that's often a collaborative output). The underlying logic: who we're for, what pain we solve, why we're different, and how to say that in language that resonates with a specific buyer. This is the hardest PMM skill and the one most often underestimated.

**Go-to-market strategy.** When a new product or feature ships, someone has to figure out the launch: which segment to target first, which channel to prioritize, what the narrative arc looks like, how sales and marketing align. That's the PMM.

**Sales enablement.** PMMs make sales reps more effective. Battle cards, competitive one-pagers, objection-handling guides, demo narratives — this is the PMM translating positioning into tools that close deals.

**Competitive intelligence.** Who are the alternatives? How do buyers evaluate them? Where do you win, where do you lose, and why? The PMM owns this continuously, not just during a launch.

**Customer research.** PMMs talk to customers. Not to sell to them — to understand them. Win/loss interviews, persona research, ICP refinement. The PMM is the voice of the market inside the company.

## Why PMMs are so often misunderstood

The role spans too many functions to own clearly. In many companies, PMMs end up either too tactical (glorified content writers making datasheets) or too diffuse (vague "strategic" contributors whose impact is hard to measure). Neither is good.

Part of the problem is that PMM outcomes are hard to attribute cleanly. If win rates go up, did the enablement work? Did messaging improve? Did the product get better? Was it the new SDR? You can't always trace the thread.

The other part is that PMMs are expensive to hire and easy to deprioritize. Startups often assume founders or demand gen can cover it. They can, for a while. Then they can't.

## The Zen thing

Here's the POV I come back to: positioning is a Zen exercise. Strip away the noise. Return to fundamentals. Who is this for? What specific pain does it address? What's the evidence that we solve it better than the alternative?

Every PMM should be able to answer those three questions about their product, cold, in a conversation, without slides. If they can't, the positioning work isn't done.

## Why this matters for you

If you're **hiring a PMM**, know what you're actually buying. You're not buying a content resource or a launch coordinator. You're buying someone who can own the strategic story of your product and make every other function more effective. Staff them accordingly, give them access to customers and data, and don't bury them under execution tasks.

If you're **aspiring to PMM**, understand that the role rewards people who are curious about customers, comfortable with ambiguity, and willing to do the unglamorous work of documentation and enablement alongside the exciting work of positioning and launch. Both are the job.

Either way: the clearer you are on what the role is, the more value you'll get from it.
