Ask ten people at your company what you do and why someone should buy it. If you get ten different answers, you don't have a positioning problem — you have a positioning *crisis*.

A positioning document is the fix. Not a deck, not a tagline, not a one-pager for sales. A real positioning document is a strategic source of truth: the single artifact every team pulls from when they describe the product, target a segment, or write a word of copy.

## What it actually is

The best framework I've found for thinking about positioning is the Value Proposition Canvas, developed by Alex Osterwalder. It has two sides.

The **Customer Profile** captures:
- **Jobs to be done** — what your customer is actually trying to accomplish
- **Pains** — the obstacles, frustrations, and risks standing in their way
- **Gains** — the outcomes and benefits they want (practical and emotional)

The **Value Map** captures:
- **Products & Services** — what you offer
- **Pain Relievers** — how you reduce or eliminate those pains
- **Gain Creators** — how you deliver the gains customers care about

**Fit** is when the two sides align. When your pain relievers directly address the stated pains, and your gain creators match what customers actually want — that's where positioning gets powerful. The positioning document makes that fit explicit and holds it across every function.

## Why it matters more than you think

Done well, a positioning document drives four real outcomes:

**Strategic Unity.** When everyone — product, sales, marketing, exec — is pulling from the same document, decisions get faster. "Should we pursue this segment?" becomes answerable. "Does this feature belong in the narrative?" becomes answerable. Without shared positioning, every meeting starts with a debate about fundamentals.

**Message Coherence.** Buyers encounter your brand across many touchpoints. If the SDR email says one thing, the website says another, and the demo says a third, they notice. Positioning gives every touchpoint a common spine.

**Operational Excellence.** The positioning document isn't just for marketing. It reduces onboarding time for new sales reps, gives content teams a brief they can actually use, and helps product prioritize what to build next. It's infrastructure.

**Market Distinction.** Knowing what makes you different — specifically, not generically — is hard to get right and easy to get lazy about. A positioning document forces the "why us, not them" answer into writing, where it can be challenged and refined.

## What goes inside it

A complete positioning document covers:

- **Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)** — firmographic and behavioral criteria for your best-fit accounts
- **Ideal Prospect Profile (IPP)** — the human buyer inside that company: their title, their concerns, their buying context
- **Personas** — the people who influence, evaluate, and use your product (not just the buyer)
- **Verticals** — which industries you're targeting, and what's distinct about each
- **Brand Overview** — who you are, what you stand for, and how you want to be perceived
- **Value Proposition** — the core promise, specific enough to be meaningful
- **Messaging Guidelines** — how to talk about the product: tone, vocabulary, what to say, what to avoid

These components don't have to live in a single massive document. Some teams use a positioning brief plus a separate messaging guide. The format matters less than the discipline of keeping it current and making it accessible to everyone who needs it.

## One last thing

A positioning document is not a one-time deliverable. The market moves. Competitors launch. Your product evolves. Your ICP sharpens as you close more deals.

Build the document. Share it widely. Review it when something significant changes — a new product line, a new competitor, a new segment you're pursuing. Treat it like the infrastructure it is: maintained, not archived.

If you've never had one, starting is the hard part. Once it exists, the question shifts from "where do we begin?" to "how do we keep it honest?" That's a much better problem to have.
